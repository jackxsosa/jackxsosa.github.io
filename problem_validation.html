Article 1

Citation

Yemets, A., Plokhovska, S., Shadrina, R., Kravets, O., & Blume, Ya. (2024). Space Research in Ukraine. 2022—2024. https://doi.org/10.15407/akademperiodyka.507.172

Summary

Understanding the response of plants to microgravity and their ability to develop stress resistance and adapt to a spaceflight environment is vital to the advancement of space biology. However, while in a spacecraft, plants are also exposed to ionizing radiation. The research in this article takes both stressors (microgravity and ionizing radiation) into account. The process of autophagy plays a crucial role in stress resistance. This study uses Arabidopsius thaliana and Pisum sativum (pea plants) as model organisms. When A. thaliana was tested in a clinostat compared to control plants, after 12 days, the autophagosomes in clinostated and control plants became practically identical. These finding support the gradual adaptation of plants in a micro-gravity simulation. Another study investigated the compared effects of microgravity and gamma radiation on autophagy and cytogenetics in pea seedlings to mimic spaceflight conditions. Overall, the study shows that gamma radiation causes dose-dependent cytogenetic and physiological changes, including enhanced autophagy and PCD. It also reveals a plateau in chromosomal aberrations at higher radiation doses, potentially reflecting homeostatic mechanisms and plant adaptation. The study also looks into melatonin's potential as a stress-reduction tool in environments similar to space. By scavenging reactive oxygen species (ROS) and controlling NO levels, melatonin improves root growth and stress resistance, which helps plants become more resilient in artificial microgravity. The study emphasizes the role that autophagy and NO signaling play in plant adaptation and proposes that modifying these pathways—perhaps with the help of substances like melatonin—might enhance plant growth in space, an essential function for long-duration space missions.

Critique

This piece of research supports that in a microgravity setting, the plants were able to adapt. It also reveals that gamma radiation causes dose-dependent cryogenic and physiological changes, including enhanced autophagy. The article also shows how melatonin can be used as a stress-reliever and improve root growth. This artifact provides validity and shows us how this problem is viable, and past successes.

Article 2

Citation

A., O., A.O., O., A.A., A., P., T.-F., A.O., O., & Uche. C., U. (2024). Simulated microgravity, indole-3-acetyl acid, and cow dung influence on Zea mays plant growth in contaminated soil. Applied Environmental Research. https://doi.org/10.35762/aer.2024028

Summary

This study explores the effects of simulated microgravity, indole-3-acetic acid (IAA), and cow dung on the growth of Zea mays (corn) in crude oil-contaminated soil. Crude oil contamination hurts plant growth tremendously. This contamination affects water and oxygen absorption, reduces chlorophyll formation, and limits overall biomass. In order to overcome this, the scientists used a clinostat to simulate microgravity conditions while also adding cow dung, a natural fertilizer, and IAA, a plant growth regulator, to contaminated soils. The objective was to evaluate if these treatments could improve plant height, chlorophyll content, and biomass production in such a stressful environment. Over the course of 12 weeks, plant growth measurements, including height, chlorophyll index, and biomass, were recorded and analyzed. The results showed that combining microgravity, IAA, and cow dung helped the plants grow significantly taller and healthier, especially when IAA and cow dung were used together. Cow dung also helped break down the crude oil by introducing microbes that degrade hydrocarbons, making the soil better for plant growth. This suggests that using these methods could be a good way to help plants grow in polluted areas, especially where there’s crude oil contamination.

Critique

This article introduces a new factor of using cow dung as fertilizer. When mixing fertilizer and IAA together in a microgravity setting, plants grew extremely healthy, even in contaminated soil. This is very helpful for our project. It shows the successes of mixing cow dung and IAA in a microgravity setting. This further confirms the validity of the project by discussing past attempts.

Researchers utilize various tools such as clinostat devices and the CRISPR/Cas9 gene-editing technique to study these effects. The article emphasizes the understanding of how plants perceive gravity and how they can sustain themselves in space. The authors suggest that future research could contribute to improving plant cultivation in space, an important factor for long-term space missions.

This article calls us to further research and testing. It agrees with our project and further confirms the need for it. Patent 1

Citation

Cheng-Shyong Lu, J. (2020, February 4). SOIL MOISTURE AUTOCONTROL SYSTEM AND ITS APPLICATIONS IN SUBSURFACE IRRIGATION , OUTER SPACE AGRICULTURAL FARM , AND CONVERTING OF DESERTS INTO ARABLE FARMS.

Summary

The patent (US 10,548,268 B1) describes a **Soil Moisture Autocontrol System** designed for use in various agricultural settings, including subsurface irrigation, space agriculture, and desert farming. The system automatically adjusts soil moisture based on the plant's needs, providing efficient irrigation and nutrient delivery while conserving water. It uses a negative pressure mechanism to control moisture levels, ensuring minimal water loss due to evaporation, runoff, or gravity. The system also includes bi-directional flow conduits, moisture diffuser probes, and a reservoir for water collection and redistribution. Applications range from traditional farming to innovative environments like space agriculture, where gravity is absent, and desert reclamation, where water conservation is critical. The system is intended to solve common issues with irrigation, such as overwatering, water loss, and nutrient delivery, while providing a scalable solution for different agricultural needs.

Critique

This patent describes a Soil Moisture Autocontrol System, which is vital for our project. We need to be able to regulate our soil as the Clinostat spins with the plant inside of it. Having this as an automatic system is vital to making our clinostat more efficient. A water reservoir is vital to keep the plant hydrated, however we need a way to constantly pump water into the clinostat while it is spinning.

Patent 2

Citation

Goodwin, T. J., & Kushman, M. J. (2020, January 16). METHODS FOR CULTURING CELLS IN AN ALTERNATING IONIC MAGNETIC RESONANCE ( AIMR ) MULTIPLE - CHAMBERED CULTURE APPARATUS.

Summary

The document is a patent application (US20200017848A1) related to **methods for culturing cells in an Alternating Ionic Magnetic Resonance (AIMR) multiple-chambered culture apparatus**. The invention provides a system and method for growing tissue-like cell assemblies in a culture environment exposed to a pulsating AIMR field. The system includes a growth module and a nutrient module, designed to simulate microgravity by randomizing the gravity vector during cell culture. This enables three-dimensional growth and better cell-to-cell interaction, enhancing cellular behavior similar to natural tissue growth. The AIMR system improves over traditional bioreactors by promoting cell growth and differentiation in a more physiologically relevant environment. It also simplifies handling through disposable, pre-sterilized modules and improves the culture of mammalian cells, tissues, or organoids. Applications include tissue engineering, regenerative medicine, and cellular research, especially for space-based or microgravity experiments. The technology aims to enhance cell growth rates, fidelity, and manipulation of genetic or physiological traits.

Critique

This patent describes an Alternating Ionic Magnetic Resonance (AIMR) apparatus. This is used to simulate a setting with radiation, such as a spacecraft. Understanding how radiation affects cell growth is crucial to the success of our project. The apparatus also simulates microgravity by randomizing the gravity vector during cell culture, which is vital to our experiment. When randomizing the gravity vector, the plant won’t know which way is up or down, which is key to simulating a space setting.

Citation

Kim, H. S., Park, J. H., Seo, J. B., Eom, S., Son, J. W., Shin, T. M., Seo, D. H., Hwang, D. H., & Kim, S. G. (2016, April 28). APPARATUS AND METHOD OF STMULATING CELLS USING 3D CLINOSTAT AND ULTRASONIC WAVE.

Summary

The document describes an apparatus and method for stimulating cells using a 3D clinostat and ultrasonic waves, to study the adverse effects of microgravity on the human body and explore countermeasures. The system includes a base plate, motorized rotating frames, a cultivating plate for cells, and an ultrasonic wave output unit. By simulating microgravity conditions, the apparatus allows for experimentation with the effects on biological tissues, such as bone density loss, which is a significant concern during long-term space missions. \ The method involves setting up the 3D clinostat, cultivating cells on a plate inside the rotating frames, and applying ultrasonic stimulation to counteract microgravity’s negative effects. This setup helps simulate space conditions, allowing researchers to develop strategies for lessening damage to the human body without requiring space travel.

Critique

This patent describes a clinostat that is used to determine effects on biological tissue while in a microgravity setting. The clinostat combats microgravity’s negative effects by applying ultrasonic stimulation. It is important to study how the plant’s cell structure would be affected over time in a microgravity setting. If a long-term solution can be figured out, astronauts can have a better food source on longer missions.

Article 1

Citation:

Summary: Understanding the response of plants to microgravity and their ability to develop stress resistance and adapt to a spaceflight environment is vital to the advancement of space biology. However, while in a spacecraft, plants are also exposed to ionizing radiation. The research in this article takes both stressors (microgravity and ionizing radiation) into account. The process of autophagy plays a crucial role in stress resistance. This study uses Arabidopsius thaliana and Pisum sativum (pea plants) as model organisms. When A. thaliana was tested in a clinostat compared to control plants, after 12 days, the autophagosomes in clinostated and control plants became practically identical. These finding support the gradual adaptation of plants in a micro-gravity simulation. Another study investigated the compared effects of microgravity and gamma radiation on autophagy and cytogenetics in pea seedlings to mimic spaceflight conditions. Overall, the study shows that gamma radiation causes dose-dependent cytogenetic and physiological changes, including enhanced autophagy and PCD. It also reveals a plateau in chromosomal aberrations at higher radiation doses, potentially reflecting homeostatic mechanisms and plant adaptation. The study also looks into melatonin's potential as a stress-reduction tool in environments similar to space. By scavenging reactive oxygen species (ROS) and controlling NO levels, melatonin improves root growth and stress resistance, which helps plants become more resilient in artificial microgravity. The study emphasizes the role that autophagy and NO signaling play in plant adaptation and proposes that modifying these pathways—perhaps with the help of substances like melatonin—might enhance plant growth in space, an essential function for long-duration space missions.

Article 2

Citation:

Summary: This study explores the effects of simulated microgravity, indole-3-acetic acid (IAA), and cow dung on the growth of Zea mays (corn) in crude oil-contaminated soil. Crude oil contamination hurts plant growth tremendously. This contamination affects water and oxygen absorption, reduces chlorophyll formation, and limits overall biomass. In order to overcome this, the scientists used a clinostat to simulate microgravity conditions while also adding cow dung, a natural fertilizer, and IAA, a plant growth regulator, to contaminated soils. The objective was to evaluate if these treatments could improve plant height, chlorophyll content, and biomass production in such a stressful environment. Over the course of 12 weeks, plant growth measurements, including height, chlorophyll index, and biomass, were recorded and analyzed. The results showed that combining microgravity, IAA, and cow dung helped the plants grow significantly taller and healthier, especially when IAA and cow dung were used together. Cow dung also helped break down the crude oil by introducing microbes that degrade hydrocarbons, making the soil better for plant growth. This suggests that using these methods could be a good way to help plants grow in polluted areas, especially where there’s crude oil contamination.

Article 3

Citation:

Summary: The document explores how plants respond to microgravity and adapt to environments where gravity is weaker than on Earth. It highlights the importance of understanding these responses for future space missions, such as potential long-term human travel and habitation on other planets. The article talks about how microgravity affects plant growth, development, and morphogenesis. This is done by disrupting physiological processes like auxin flow, which is essential for plant orientation and growth in relation to gravity. Researchers utilize various tools such as clinostat devices and the CRISPR/Cas9 gene-editing technique to study these effects. The article emphasizes the understanding of how plants perceive gravity and how they can sustain themselves in space. The authors suggest that future research could contribute to improving plant cultivation in space, an important factor for long-term space missions.
